College During the Coronavirus

comparing COVID-19 data across the University System of Maryland

by Emily Riley

If you’ve been a college student at some point in your life, you’re no stranger to the common cold. Whether you got a bug in your 200-person lecture, while picking up a bite to eat in a crowded dining hall, at a party with friends or from your not-so-clean roommate, colleges across the nation are notorious for germs.

However with the coronavirus vastly changing what day-to-day activities look like for the typical student, college campuses have come to resemble ghost towns. Sporting events have limited capacity, residence halls are only offering single rooms and in-person classes are few and far between.

As students struggle to adapt to an online world, their universities also have to figure out how to handle cases. Tuition prices remain sky-high and the end of the virus is out of sight. This leaves parents and students alike wondering the same question: How will universities prioritize safety and enforce transparency with students?

According to research from the New York Times, “more than 50 colleges have reported at least 1,000 cases over the course of the pandemic, and more than 400 colleges have reported at least 100 cases.” Maryland is no different.

The University System of Maryland represents Maryland’s public universities. In an effort to better understand how schools have been handling COVID-19, I analyzed data from four of the system’s most prominent schools: the University of Maryland, College Park, the University of Maryland Baltimore County, Towson University and Salisbury University. All four universities opened this fall.

Located in the heart of Prince George’s County, just a 15-minute drive from the nation’s capital is the University of Maryland, College Park. UMD has had the most cases compared to the other schools analyzed at 609 cases of COVID-19 during their fall semester, according to the University's coronavirus dashboard.


Although the school is home to nearly 30,000 undergraduate students, their campus density was only at 22 percent last week (November 29), which reflects the already decreased amount of students living on-campus, even less due to the fact that students couldn't return to school if they went home for Thanksgiving break.


Data from the school’s COVID-19 dashboard reflects a steep downward trend since a peak in early September. The school has a positivity rate of just .6 percent this week. This makes it one one of the lowest weekly positivity rating among the other USM schools analyzed.


For more information about UMD, visit their COVID-19 dashboard.

Just about 20 minutes North of Baltimore City, Towson University has reported at least 527 cases this semester, according to their coronavirus dashboard.


Towson racks up the highest positivity rate of all four schools that I studied at 3.03 percent. Although showing a relatively high positivity rate, their weekly positivity rate was at 2.13 percent, a significant decrease from their overall average. Towson's amount of cases is also only 82 less than the University of Maryland, even though having a significantly smaller student population.
Even though at significantly higher rate than the four other universities, Towson is still undeniably lower than Baltimore County, which has a positivity rate of 7.5 percent.


For more information about Towson, visit their COVID-19 dashboard.

A smaller university on the Eastern Shore, Salisbury University has had at least 578 cases during their fall semester, according to their coronavirus dashboard.

Salisbury’s cumulative cases are higher than Towson and only 31 away from UMD, which are nearly two and three times bigger, respectively.


Their numbers reflect a sharp increase in cases following students’ return to campus in late August and early September, but the university has leveled out significantly since.


Salisbury’s positivity rate this past week was .9 percent, which is much lower than their overall averag. The school has also administered at least 28,755 tests since August 11. The university is also significantly lower than the positivity rate of Wicomico County, which has a rate of 5.96 percent.


For more information on Salisbury, visit their COVID-19 dashboard.

Baltimore County Positivity Rate

Under a 20 minute drive from Baltimore City, the University of Maryland, Baltimore County has maintained a cumulative positivity rate of .7 percent, staying far below most other universities and the county as a whole.


Although a medium-sized university, UMBC has maintained shockingly low amounts of cases, with only 58 reported this fall semester, according to their coronavirus dashboard.


Whether a result of only 1,276 students living in residence halls this semester or reflective of their testing policy, the campus seems to be the most successful at keeping their bubble of cases to a minimum. Although maintaining a relatively low positivity rate, the school saw a sharp increase in the past week to a 2.5% positivity rate. This could be possibly due to students bringing the virus back on campus with them after traveling for the Thanksgiving holiday.


For more information on UMBC, visit their COVID-19 dashboard.
